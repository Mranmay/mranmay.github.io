﻿Task for Microns Project Week 1 
1. The Four Primary Families of Inhibition
In the brain, neurons are generally divided into excitatory (the "gas pedal" that sends signals) and inhibitory (the "brakes" that regulate signals). While inhibitory neurons make up only about 15–20% of the population, they are incredibly diverse. Your task focuses on four main families:
* Parvalbumin (PV): These act like local traffic controllers. They target the main body (soma) of other neurons to provide fast, precise timing control.
* Somatostatin (SST): These are "feedback specialists". They target the far reaches of other neurons' branches to control the overall flow of information.
* VIP: These are "disinhibitory specialists." Their main job is to inhibit the inhibitors (specifically SST neurons), which effectively "releases the brakes" on the circuit.
* Lamp5/Sncg: These often include "neurogliaform cells" that release inhibitory chemicals broadly into the surrounding space rather than at a single specific point.
2. Focus on Martinotti Cells (SST)
To check if a neuron is truly a Martinotti Cell, you must look at its morphology (its physical shape):
* Dendrites (Inputs): In Martinotti cells, these are usually small, dense, and "aspiny" (meaning they lack the little bumps or "spines" common on excitatory neurons).
* Axons (Outputs): This is the key "smoking gun." An MC will have a primary axon that goes UP (ascending) toward the very top layer of the brain (Layer 1). Once there, it fans out like a horizontal net or "plexus".
* The Contrast: In excitatory neurons, the axons usually go DOWN and appear much more sparse because they are trying to reach distant brain areas rather than fanning out locally.
2. Density and "Marginal" Distributions
Turn a messy 3D neuron into a "presentable figure," by using statistical plots to describe where the cell's output is concentrated.
* 2D Axonal Density: Imagine looking down on the neuron or looking at it from the side. You are creating a map where the colour represents how "thick" or "dense" the axonal branches are in that specific spot.
* Marginal Distribution (Marginals): This is like looking at the "shadow" of the neuron projected onto a single axis.
   * Marginal across Depth: You "squash" the neuron's 3D structure into a 1D line that shows how many axonal nodes (points on the skeleton) exist at each depth of the cortex. For a Martinotti cell, you should see a big peak at the top (Layer 1) where the axon fans out.
* Axonal Span in X: This measures the horizontal reach of the cell. You calculate this by finding the distance between the furthest left node and the furthest right node on the X-axis. Martinotti cells often have a very wide span—sometimes over 300 micrometres—to influence many neighbouring columns of neurons.
3. Why "Kill" SST Neurons? (Ablation)
The task includes a rationale for understanding the effect of "killing" (ablating) these neurons.
* The "Blanket of Inhibition": Because Martinotti cells fan out so widely in Layer 1, they are thought to provide a "blanket of inhibition" that keeps the brain's activity from spiralling out of control.
* Surround Suppression: These cells help the brain focus. By inhibiting a wide area, they "suppress" the signals from the "surround" so the brain can focus on a specific visual object.
* Bridging with Connectomics: By removing an SST neuron in a computer model, you can see how the "plumbing" of the brain changes. If you know a cell's axonal span is 200 micrometres, you can predict that killing it will affect any neighbouring neurons within that 200-micrometre radius. You are essentially using the physical map (connectomics) to explain the functional "blackout" caused by losing that cell.
4. Summary of Visual Tasks
To finish your week, the figures should "tell a story":
1. Plot one example neuron: Show its small dendrites and its dramatic "upward" axon.
2. Plot Density: Show the "cloud" of where its axon reaches.
3. Plot Marginals: Show the peak of activity in Layer 1.
4. Stats: Calculate the average horizontal span (X) and standard density to show how far its influence reaches across the cortex.

2. Quantitative Analysis of Neurons
Beyond visual maps, specific statistical measures for individual neurons:
* Gaussian Fitting: Fit a Gaussian distribution to each neuron's spatial data.
* Standard Deviation and FWHM: Measure the standard deviation from these fits and plot a histogram of the Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM) across the neuron distribution.
* Radial Distance / Depth Plot: For each row in your analysis matrix (grouped by layer), first plot synapse distributions as a function of radial distance from the soma and depth. Use unsigned radial distance values. This comes before splitting inputs into basal vs. apical dendrites.
3. Layer-Specific Connectivity Matrix
"Put together" the two canonical circuits by mapping how inputs from different layers are distributed on the dendritic trees of single neurons.
* Build the Matrix: Create a matrix where each row represents a postsynaptic class (e.g., L2/3, L4, L5) and each column represents a presynaptic source.
* Compare Input Distributions: First compare the distribution of synapses as a function of radial distance from the soma and cortical depth for each input class. Only after this split inputs into basal vs. apical dendrites and repeat the same comparison.
* Subtraction Maps: Continue the "game" of pairwise subtractions (e.g., Layer 2/3 minus Layer 4) to highlight where specific inputs are more or less abundant than expected.
4. Focused Layers and Reading

* Calculate Delta Orientation (Delta ORI): For each post-synaptic neuron, calculate the difference between its preferred orientation and the preferred orientation of each of its pre-synaptic inputs.
* Center the Distributions: Create histograms (preferably plotted as curves) of these differences, shifting them so that the post-synaptic cell’s preferred orientation is always at zero.
* Handle Circular Measures: Because orientation is periodic (0–180 degrees), use a modulus operation in the code to ensure differences remain within the correct range (e.g., -90 to 90 degrees).

Task for Microns Project Week 5
1. Improve the pyramidal density plots
Redo the pyramidal-cell density plots from the previous analysis, but with cleaner spatial resolution and better soma alignment.
* Change the bin size: Use 10 × 10 µm bins instead of 25 × 25 µm bins for the density maps. This should make the maps higher resolution while keeping the same biological question: where are synapses distributed around L2/3, L4, and L5 pyramidal cells?
* Crop the depth range: For the density plots, cut the plotted depth range at around 600 µm. This keeps the focus on the cortical region where the relevant pyramidal-cell somas and inputs are located, instead of showing too much empty or misleading lower-volume space.
* Soma-centered plots: Repeat the same density analysis but with the somas aligned. Somas should be centered on the x-axis, on the y/depth axis, and on both x and y together. The x/y-centered version is important because it ignores absolute depth from pia and asks a different question: what does the input distribution look like around the soma itself?
* Height marginals: Along with the 2D density plots, generate height/depth marginal plots. The old version used depth from pia, but for the soma-centered version the height marginal should be relative to the soma. This will help compare whether inputs are mainly above, below, or around the soma for different pyramidal classes.
2. Receptive Field analysis
Start a new analysis connecting the MICRONS anatomical/connectomic data to visual function. The goal is to move from physical cortical coordinates to visual-field coordinates, using each cell’s receptive field location.
* Use azimuth and elevation: For functionally matched neurons, retrieve the receptive-field position of each cell in terms of azimuth and elevation/altitude. These are not the same as anatomical x/y/z coordinates. Anatomical x/y/z describes where the neuron is in cortex, while azimuth/elevation describes where in visual space the neuron responds.
* Compare pre- and post-synaptic RFs: For each postsynaptic pyramidal neuron, identify its presynaptic input neurons that also have functional receptive-field information. Then compare the receptive field of the postsynaptic neuron to the receptive fields of its inputs. 
* Make RF-centered input maps: For one example postsynaptic cell, plot the receptive-field centers of its presynaptic partners in Δazimuth/Δelevation space. Then repeat this across many postsynaptic cells and pool the centered input distributions to create a population receptive-field input map. If connectivity is functionally organized, the presynaptic RFs should cluster near zero instead of being randomly spread across visual space.
3. Layer-specific receptive-field structure
Once the basic RF analysis works, repeat it separately for different presynaptic and postsynaptic layer combinations.
* Start with pyramidal cells: Begin with functionally matched excitatory pyramidal cells, especially L2/3 postsynaptic cells, because this is the cleanest continuation from the previous orientation-selectivity analysis.
* Compare input layers: Split the presynaptic inputs by layer, for example L2/3, L4, and L5 inputs. Then ask whether different layers provide inputs from different regions of visual space. This links the receptive-field analysis to the layer-specific connectivity matrix from Week 2.
* Connect to dendritic targeting: If time permits, combine receptive-field similarity with synapse location on the postsynaptic neuron. For example, compare RF distance for inputs targeting basal dendrites versus apical dendrites, or proximal versus distal dendritic regions. This asks whether different dendritic compartments receive inputs representing different parts of visual space.
